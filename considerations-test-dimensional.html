Core Question: Which programming activities should students do without AI assistance to develop essential skills?

Task/Activity AI Use? Pedagogical Reasoning
Writing initial code solutions
 AI shouldn't Problem decomposition and algorithmic thinking develop through the struggle of translating ideas to code. AI-generated solutions bypass this cognitive work.
Debugging logic errors
 AI shouldn't Debugging builds systematic reasoning about program behavior. Tracing execution, forming hypotheses, and testing fixes are core CS skills.
Syntax error checking
 AI could Syntax is low-level and can distract from learning higher-order concepts. AI can help students focus on logic and design rather than semicolons.

 AI could Cryptic compiler errors can block learning. AI explanations can scaffold understanding, especially for beginners encountering new error types.
Code documentation
 AI could AI can draft comments/docstrings, but students should review for accuracy. Learning to document is valuable, but AI can speed this without bypassing core learning.
Exploring alternative approaches
 AI could After solving a problem, seeing alternative implementations can deepen understanding. AI can expose students to different patterns and techniques.
Writing test cases
 AI shouldn't Identifying edge cases and designing tests requires understanding the problem space. This thinking shouldn't be outsourced to AI.

Task/Activity AI Use? Pedagogical Reasoning
Close reading of texts
 AI shouldn't Noticing diction, syntax, imagery, and form requires sustained attention to language. AI summaries bypass the cognitive work of reading closely.
Developing original arguments
 AI shouldn't Constructing interpretations grounded in textual evidence is core to literary thinking. AI-generated thesis statements don't develop this skill.
Drafting analytical essays
 AI shouldn't Writing is thinking. The struggle to articulate ideas develops analytical voice. AI drafts prevent this developmental process.
Brainstorming essay topics
 AI could AI can help students unstuck when beginning an essay, but students should evaluate which ideas are worth pursuing based on their reading.
Grammar and style editing
 AI could After drafting their own work, students can use AI for proofreading. This helps them focus revision efforts on argument and analysis.
Finding textual evidence
 AI shouldn't Selecting which passages support claims requires judgment about textual meaning. This interpretive work is core to literary analysis.
Citation formatting
 AI could Citation mechanics (MLA/Chicago format) are technical. AI can handle formatting while students focus on analysis and argumentation.

Core Question: Which lab and problem-solving activities require hands-on practice vs. computational assistance?

Task/Activity AI Use? Pedagogical Reasoning
Laboratory technique
 AI shouldn't Physical skills (pipetting, titration, equipment setup) require hands-on practice. AI instructions can't develop manual dexterity and lab intuition.
Proposing reaction mechanisms
 AI shouldn't Predicting how molecules react requires chemical intuition about electron flow and reactivity. This reasoning must be developed, not outsourced.
Troubleshooting failed experiments
 AI shouldn't Diagnosing why reactions didn't work (impure reagents? wrong temperature?) builds chemical reasoning. Students need practice reasoning backward from results.
Molecular structure prediction
 AI could Computational chemistry tools are standard in research. After learning principles by hand, students can use software for complex modeling.
Spectroscopy interpretation
 AI shouldn't Learning to read NMR, IR, and mass spectra builds pattern recognition. Students need practice connecting peaks to molecular features.
Literature searches
 AI could AI can help find relevant papers and extract key findings, allowing students to focus on understanding chemistry rather than database navigation.
Data visualization
 AI could After collecting and analyzing data, AI tools can help create publication-quality figures. This doesn't bypass core chemical reasoning.

Core Question: Which historical research and writing activities develop critical thinking that AI would bypass?

Task/Activity AI Use? Pedagogical Reasoning
Evaluating primary sources
 AI shouldn't Learning to assess credibility, bias, and perspective requires direct engagement with documents. AI summaries remove the interpretive work.
Understanding historical context
 AI shouldn't Grasping social, political, and cultural conditions requires reading widely and making connections. This contextual thinking can't be shortcut.
Constructing historical arguments
 AI shouldn't Building interpretations that acknowledge complexity and alternative views is core historical practice. AI-generated arguments lack this nuance.
Finding secondary sources
 AI could AI can help locate relevant scholarship, allowing students to spend time reading and evaluating sources rather than searching databases.
Chronology and factual background
 AI could Basic timeline and contextual facts can come from AI, freeing students to focus on interpretation and argument rather than fact-gathering.
Citation checking
 AI could Verifying citation format and completeness is mechanical work. AI assistance allows students to focus on historical reasoning.
Identifying historiographical debates
 AI shouldn't Understanding how historians interpret events differently requires reading competing arguments. This comparative analysis must be done by students.

Core Question: Which analytical activities build statistical reasoning vs. computational efficiency?

Task/Activity AI Use? Pedagogical Reasoning
Implementing algorithms from scratch
 AI shouldn't You don't understand gradient descent or k-means until you've coded it yourself. Libraries abstract away the learning.
Selecting appropriate methods
 AI shouldn't Choosing regression vs. classification, or which algorithm fits the problem, requires statistical reasoning. AI suggestions don't build this judgment.
Interpreting model outputs
 AI shouldn't Understanding what results mean, their uncertainty, and limitations requires statistical thinking. AI can describe outputs but not teach interpretation.
Data cleaning and preprocessing
 AI could After understanding data types and missing value strategies, AI can help with tedious cleaning code. But students should understand what's happening.
Visualization creation
 AI could AI can generate plotting code efficiently. Students should focus on choosing appropriate visualizations and interpreting what they reveal.
Identifying biases in data
 AI shouldn't Recognizing sampling bias, confounding, and fairness issues requires critical thinking about how data was collected and what it represents.
Using established libraries
 AI could After implementing core methods by hand, AI can help with library syntax (sklearn, pandas). This focuses learning on concepts rather than API details.
